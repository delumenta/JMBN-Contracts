JMBN – BMT Training Manual

BMT Manual – Fire Team Movements

Certification Track: BMT – Basic Military Training
Ref: FTM-01 Version: 1.0 (Training) Audience: Recruits / Fire Team Leaders
Sections
1
Aim & Scope
What this lesson covers and what “good” looks like.

This section sets the intent for Fire Team Movements in JMBN’s BMT track. By the end of this module, every recruit should be able to:

  • Describe the basic roles within a four-person fire team.
  • Select an appropriate formation based on terrain and threat.
  • Apply the three core movement techniques during patrol.
  • React correctly to enemy contact at team level.
Training standard
A recruit is considered proficient when they can move as part of a team with correct spacing, maintain sector discipline, and respond to basic commands without needing constant correction.

Fire Team Movements in JMBN are based on real-world doctrine, adapted for:

  • Game environments (Star Citizen FPS & VBSS style ops)
  • Latency and visibility limitations
  • Small-unit organisation inside JMBN platoons
2
Fire Team Structure & Roles
Who does what inside a 4-man element.

A standard JMBN fire team is 4 members. This is the basic building block for squad and platoon operations.

2.1 Roles

  • Team Leader (TL) – overall control, decides formation, movement, and contact drills.
  • Point / Lead Rifle – front security, first eyes on contact, sets pace.
  • Support / Gunner – main firepower, covers key sectors during halts and contact.
  • Rear Security – watches the back arc, ensures no one is left behind, manages spacing.
Instructor note
In game, call out your role at the start of the patrol: “Vex – Team Lead, Kara – Point, Malik – Gunner, Rasa – Rear.”

2.2 Sector Responsibility

  • Point: 10 to 2 o’clock.
  • TL: floats, fills gaps, usually 11 to 1 o’clock.
  • Gunner: flank emphasis (9 or 3 o’clock, as assigned).
  • Rear: 4 to 8 o’clock, plus spacing and stragglers.
3
Standard Fire Team Formations
Shape of the team in space.

Formations are the pattern your team maintains while moving. They control spacing, fields of fire, and reaction to contact.

Summary – when to use what
File
Tight terrain, corridors
Wedge
Open ground, unknown threat
Line
Assault to front
Stag. Column
Road / hallway, moderate threat

3.1 File

Description: All members in a single line, one behind another.

  • Use when: terrain is narrow (stairs, corridors, dense clutter).
  • Strengths: easy control, simple sectors.
  • Weaknesses: limited firepower to the front, vulnerable to flanks.

3.2 Wedge

Description: Point in front, two in the middle offset left/right, rear at the back.

  • Use when: moving across open terrain or large rooms.
  • Strengths: good all-round security, flexible.
  • Weaknesses: needs space and discipline to maintain.

3.3 Line

Description: All members in a line abreast, shoulder-to-shoulder (with spacing).

  • Use when: preparing to deliver maximum fire to the front.
  • Strengths: maximum frontal firepower.
  • Weaknesses: weak flanks, hard to control in tight terrain.

3.4 Staggered Column

Description: Two loose files offset left and right of a path/road/hallway.

  • Use when: moving along a street, corridor, or ship passageway.
  • Strengths: better fields of fire, some depth.
  • Weaknesses: requires coordination, can bunch under stress.
BMT standard
Recruits must be able to identify and move in File and Wedge on command. Line and Staggered Column may be introduced during later phases.
4
Movement Techniques
How aggressive or cautious the team moves.

Movement techniques describe how the team moves, regardless of formation: how fast, how spread, and how ready to fight.

4.1 Traveling

  • Threat Level: low
  • Spacing: slightly closer (to avoid losing visual).
  • Use when: time matters more than immediate contact (safe areas).

4.2 Traveling Overwatch

  • Threat Level: medium / unknown.
  • Formation: same as normal, but slightly more spacing.
  • Behavior: Point is more cautious, team pauses more often to scan.

4.3 Bounding Overwatch

Bounding means part of the team moves while the other part covers.

  • Threat Level: high or expected contact.
  • Use when: closing in on a known enemy location, crossing danger areas.

Basic bounding sequence (two-element team):

  1. Element A covers; Element B moves forward to next position.
  2. Element B calls “Set”.
  3. Element B covers; Element A moves past B or up to new position.
  4. Repeat until objective is reached.
BMT drill phrase
For training, use clear calls: “Alpha set.”, “Bravo moving.” Keep it short and consistent.
5
Contact Drills (Fire Team Level)
Immediate reactions when rounds start coming in.

Contact drills must be automatic. When someone calls “Contact front!”, every member knows the default reaction without waiting for a long briefing.

5.1 Contact Front (Hasty)

  1. Immediate action: nearest to contact returns fire, everyone moves to cover.
  2. Team Leader: confirms direction, gives a simple order: “Team – line, suppress front.”
  3. Gunner: establishes main base of fire.
  4. Rear: checks flanks and rear, then joins main fire sector.

5.2 Contact Flank (Left/Right)

  • Nearest member to that flank calls “Contact left!” or “Contact right!”.
  • Team pivots formation to face the threat while seeking cover.
  • TL decides to either fix (suppress) or break contact.

5.3 Break Contact (Basic)

  1. Team leader calls “Break contact – smoke / backwards / towards X.”
  2. One element fires while the other bounds back to the next cover position.
  3. Elements alternate movement until they reach a safer line or rally point.
Critical safety
Never move in front of a friendly who is already firing a weapon. In training, if someone crosses your muzzle, call “Check fire!” and reset the drill.
6
Communication & Signals
Keeping a team together without cluttering comms.

Clear comms prevent confusion and team wipes. For BMT Fire Team Movements, we standardise a small set of voice commands and hand signals.

6.1 Standard Voice Commands

  • “Set” – I’m in position and ready to cover.
  • “Moving” – I am leaving cover and moving now.
  • “Last man” – I am the last in the team; once I pass, move or close the formation.
  • “Contact front/left/right/rear” – immediate threat direction.
  • “Hold” – stop where you are, stay covered.

6.2 Basic Hand Signals (for reference)

In game, hand signals may be symbolic. As a training standard:

  • Move out: Arm extended forward, hand waving in a “come on” motion.
  • Halt: Hand straight up, palm forward.
  • Enemy spotted: Point weapon or finger towards contact, then show number of enemies with fingers.
JMBN practice
Use voice first (because of VOIP), but leaders are encouraged to gesture in game to reinforce directions where possible.
7
Safety & Common Errors
Things that break teams – and how to avoid them.

7.1 Safety Rules (Training)

  • Do not fire past or through your team mates unless you have a clear line and permission.
  • If you’re unsure of a target, ask or hold fire.
  • Call out reloads if you are in a critical position: “Gunner reloading!”.

7.2 Common Errors

  • Bunching up: standing too close, one grenade kills the team.
  • Over-extending: point man runs too far ahead, breaks comms and support.
  • Silent movement: nobody says “Moving / Set,” so no one knows who is covering.
  • Fighting the formation: ignoring spacing or leaving assigned sector.
Correction drill
On repeated errors, instructors may call a “freeze” and reset the team to the last safe position, then walk through the correction slowly.
8
Practical Drills & Assessment Checklist
How this is trained and what is examined for BMT.

8.1 Recommended Drill Sequence

  1. Dry Walk: Team moves in File across a simple route, no enemies.
  2. Wedge Practice: Team practices wedge in open area, adjusting spacing on the move.
  3. Traveling vs Traveling Overwatch: Instructor calls threat changes; team adjusts tempo.
  4. Bounding Pairs: 2-man teams practice “Set / Moving” across a short lane.
  5. Contact Front Drill: Instructor triggers contact; team executes standard reaction.

8.2 BMT Assessment Points (Instructor Guide)

  • Recruit maintains assigned position in the formation.
  • Recruit keeps reasonable spacing and does not constantly bunch up.
  • Recruit reacts correctly to “Halt”, “Move”, and “Contact front”.
  • Recruit uses simple voice procedure: announces “Moving” and “Set” when bounding.
  • Recruit does not cross friendly muzzle lines during drills.
Certification tie-in
Successful completion of this module contributes to the BMT certification. In the JMBN system, instructors record completion under the recruit’s profile once they demonstrate these skills in at least one controlled exercise.
Lesson Overview
  • Size: 4-man fire team.
  • Key shapes: File, Wedge, Line.
  • Techniques: Traveling, Overwatch, Bounding.
  • Core words: Moving / Set / Contact.
Formation Selection (Quick)

Open Terrain → Wedge

Tight / Corridor → File

Assault Front → Line

Road / Hallway → Stag. Column

Instructor Notes
  • Start slower than you think; speed can be added later.
  • Correct spacing and sectors before adding enemy AI/players.
  • Use a fixed script for first few runs to build muscle memory.
  • Encourage recruits to call out mistakes on themselves.
Debrief Prompts
  • “Where did we bunch up?”
  • “When did we lose eyes on each other?”
  • “Who felt uncovered during movement?”
  • “Were ‘Moving’ / ‘Set’ calls clear?”
